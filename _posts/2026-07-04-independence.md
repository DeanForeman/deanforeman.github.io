---
Independence
---


In April, I started a creative writing project, a blog, if you will. The choice was the synthesis of millions upon millions of stimuli over the course of my life, though, most immediately, it was inspired by my reading of Mary Roach’s _Stiff_, Lindy West’s _The Witches are Coming_, Michelle Alexander’s _The New Jim Crow_, and a revisit to _Fahrenheit 451_ by Ray Bradbury. I was restless.

I was reminded of my Pampo’s protests in the 60s and 70s, the voice he raised with the members of _la Raza_—his people, my people—in the hopes of achieving—what was it again?—oh, equanimity; _legal_ equanimity in the United States; a commitment from the United States government that their rights as citizens were ensured, and that local municipalities and their police would preserve those freedoms; secure their rights, not impair them.
 
Two generations ago, my grandfather, at approximately the age I am now, had to picket, yell, _bleed_ against the white hegemonic power structure to prove his intrinsic citizenship. His American birth could not earn him fair treatment, nor could the sacred Constitution. It was demanded that _la Raza_ submit to the customs of the White Picket Fence should it want the blessings of liberty. 

But to wake every day, a human that bleeds red, and be told your food smells “weird”, that the seats in the back of the theater are meant for you; that you walk home to the poor part of town—how that’s the reason the police officers stop you; why one holds you to the alley wall as the other searches your book bag, obviously finding nothing, leaving you disheveled in the dirt with the remnants of your dignity—could you handle it, reader, as an elementary student? Might you have, upon reaching political maturity, picketed, yelled, _bled_ to proclaim your rights—your ordained freedoms—against police officers with batons and tear gas?

The United States put _our_ Chicano ancestors, their fellow citizens, in a social cage, banged their heads on the bars by segregating public spaces and neighborhoods, then questioned how _la Raza_ could be so resistant in embracing Anglo culture. 

But we’re wiser now, kinder, and it’s clearer today more than ever that a multitude of cultures and peoples existing at once, working in synergy, is not merely a productive means for increasing commerce, it’s our future, our legacy; equanimity for all humanity is our mission and our mantra. It’s not impossible, dear reader, we just haven’t been shown the dream. 

We’re walking the path of equanimity presently—the passage of the 14th Amendment, women’s suffrage, the protests and marches for civil rights in the wake of domestic terrorism, the United Nations’ recognition of Indian rights and sovereignty, the national, then global, protests against police brutality and militarization following the murder of George Floyd, the present outcry against human erasure in Palestine—**all significant**, all things surely thought improbable, if not impossible, by their firsthand witnesses. 

Our ancestors marked their trails, noting where they fell and where their paths ended, and their efforts attest to the great power human love and endurance have in shaping the world, the potential _we_ have to make it a safer planet for our neighbors and peers. It is possible.

--Dean Diego
