Sound and Voice
---

I have a Discord server that I share with my two best friends from high school, and one of them sent a message to the ‘shower-thoughts’ channel (yes) saying, “I’ve always clocked that you know and have known actor names even from a very young age. Do you remember how you learned their names? I always remember the characters over the actors.”

I remember watching movies with my dad and my grandpa. Conflict flowed easily between the two of them, but they always waved a quiet flag of truce when it came to movies. They had their preferences, mind you—my dad loved sci fi space operas, and my grandpa preferred historical slow burns—but they would often cede to one another’s tastes. They catalogued their viewings with a ritualistic barking, treated as sacred as the digital sound itself. 

“Hmmm, dad, y’see how young Tommy Lee looks there? When did this one come out?”

My grandpa would check using the Info button on his universal remote and reply, “1997.”

“Ninety seven,” my dad would repeat, his eyes glazing over as he visited a past long gone. Slowly shaking his head, he’d ask, “How long was that after The Fugitive?”

“Harrison Ford? That was… ninety three, I reckon.”

“Hmmm, yeah.” Hollywood filled the empty sound. Then, with his eyes still on the screen, my grandpa would say, “See he’s got a new one coming out, Tommy Lee? *No Country for Old Men*.”

“I hadn’t seen that.”

“It’s got that other gentleman in it—oh, goodness—from the W. Bush picture…”

“Oh, umm—Brolin?”

“Brolin—yes! I saw a commercial for it the other day. You ever read Cormac McCarthy? *The Road*? Anyway, it’s off one of his books. We oughta keep an eye on it.”

They returned their attention to the screen, watching the movie but also watching something else—something beyond the pixels, just past their vision. Digital noise, then temporal weaving. Movies were not stagnant; they were alive, like flames stirred by a ghost story around a campfire. And the flames could share a separate narrative with those tuned to it: a narrative that captured the greater context of cinema and human creativity, culture and civil unrest; the parts we love to play and the parts we wish we hadn’t.

In late elementary school, after seeing the original *Star Wars* trilogy, I became more aware that movies and shows were fictional performances by actors. Previously, I had believed that death scenes in movies were happening in real time as I watched them. The Iron Giant marched once more toward sacrifice in his titular film, unwavering even on my fifth viewing; how permanent was the Iron Giant’s sacrifice, exactly? Oohhhhhh.

The *Star Wars* end credits were an aural treat, and they helped burn the actors’ names into my memory. But *Star Wars* was an exception among the things I usually watched, since I almost exclusively preferred animated stories. In animation, an actor’s performance is masked behind layers of design, planning, and drawing—this is largely the point, right? Nickelodeon doesn’t want you to hear Tom Kenny on the screen; they want you to hear SpongeBob. That’s the illusion. Yet, in the vein of my grandpa and dad before me, I was catching actors in their different work—not with my eyes, but with my ears.

I’ve long thought my ears were somewhat more sensitive. I used to listen to how my dad’s footsteps fell on the hardwood and then the carpet, discerning in a second whether he was intoxicated and whether he intended to make trouble for me.

I’ve since learned—amidst my youth, at least—that voice acting was a tight knit community of TV and theatre folks who found themselves in recording booths between gigs. Unionization helped solidify those bonds. Sometimes it was hard not to notice how frequently certain actors appeared: Tara Strong, Carolyn Lawrence, Phil LaMarr, Dee Bradley Baker—hundreds of hours dedicated to their craft, parts large and thousands more small, fleshing out the worlds animators had been dreaming.

Looking back, I can’t pinpoint the moment I started caring about actors, or when I began recognizing them by sound alone. It wasn’t a deliberate practice. It wasn’t study. It was something quieter, something absorbed. I grew up in a living room where movies were watched with ears as much as eyes, where voices carried memory and meaning, where the people behind the sound mattered even when I didn’t know their names.

Maybe that’s the thread that runs through it all: the sense that stories aren’t just seen but heard, and that somewhere in the overlap—between the voices on the screen and the ones in the room—I learned to listen closely enough that the actors never stayed anonymous for long.

— Dean Diego
